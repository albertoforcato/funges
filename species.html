Wild Edibles on Funges

These are the wild plants, berries, nuts and mushrooms currently featured on the map. Each has specific growing conditions, and their presence is inferred from environmental data.

Amaranth (Amaranthus spp.)

A highly versatile plant with vibrant green or red leaves and tiny protein-rich seeds. The young leaves can be used like spinach, while the seeds are a traditional grain substitute packed with nutrients. Foragers prize it for its dual use — leafy greens and pseudo-cereal — often thriving in disturbed soils and open sunny areas.

Wild Asparagus (Asparagus acutifolius)

Slender green spears pushing through the soil in early spring mark the arrival of wild asparagus. Unlike its cultivated cousin, it grows among shrubs and hedges, especially near trails. Its tender shoots offer a concentrated flavor and are one of the earliest foraged vegetables of the year.

Black Chanterelle (Craterellus cornucopioides)

Nicknamed “horn of plenty,” this elusive mushroom has a smoky black hue and trumpet shape. It thrives in damp leaf litter under beech and oak trees. With a rich aroma and excellent shelf life when dried, it's a prized ingredient in broths and sauces, making the effort of finding it well worth it.

Chestnut (Castanea sativa)

Sweet, starchy nuts encased in spiky husks, dropping from towering chestnut trees in autumn. They’ve been a staple in mountain diets for centuries — eaten roasted, milled into flour, or boiled. Chestnuts offer energy-rich sustenance and are abundant in traditional chestnut groves.

Chickweed (Stellaria media)

A delicate green carpet of edible leaves and starry white flowers. Chickweed is easy to identify and grows almost year-round in moist, nutrient-rich soil. Its mild flavor makes it a go-to wild salad green and an accessible entry point for urban and rural foragers alike.

Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale)

One of the most widespread and versatile plants in the wild. Every part — leaf, flower, and root — is edible and medicinal. Leaves are bitter and rich in minerals, roots can be roasted as a coffee alternative, and the flowers are used in syrups and infusions. It’s a resilient gift from nature.

Wild Garlic (Allium ursinum)

Aromatic green leaves and white blossoms that carpet forest floors in spring. Also known as ramsons, this plant emits a strong garlic scent and grows in moist, shaded areas. It’s easy to recognize and brings a garlicky kick to pestos, oils, and spreads — a springtime treasure.

Larch Cones (Larix decidua)

The only deciduous conifer in Europe, larch sheds its needles in winter and produces small woody cones. While not a direct food source, the young cones are foraged for their resinous properties and used in medicinal syrups or flavor extracts in traditional herbal recipes.

Lingonberry (Vaccinium vitis-idaea)

Tiny ruby-red berries growing low on evergreen shrubs in boreal forests and alpine heaths. Tart and high in antioxidants, they’re traditionally made into preserves or sauces. Lingonberries thrive in acidic soils and often grow alongside wild blueberries and mosses.

Masterwort (Peucedanum ostruthium)

A perennial from mountainous regions with aromatic leaves and umbels of white or pinkish flowers. Historically used as both food and folk medicine. It can be found in alpine meadows and pastures and has a long tradition in herbal liqueurs and rustic dishes of central Europe.

Morel (Morchella esculenta)

Among the most sought-after wild mushrooms, easily recognized by their honeycomb cap and hollow body. Appears briefly in spring, especially after rain, in forests, riverbanks, and old burns. With an earthy, nutty flavor, morels are a delicacy that reward those who know where to look.

Porcini (Boletus edulis)

Also called “king bolete,” this robust mushroom grows under pine, spruce, and oak. It has a firm white stem and a smooth brown cap, with sponge-like pores underneath. Porcini are rich in umami and highly valued fresh or dried — one of Europe’s iconic wild edibles.

Nettle (Urtica dioica)

Known for its sting, but also for its high iron and vitamin content. Nettle thrives in rich, damp soils and can be found near streams, barns, and trails. Once cooked or dried, it loses its sting and becomes a powerhouse for soups, teas, and herbal tonics.

Parasol Mushroom (Macrolepiota procera)

Tall and unmistakable, with a snakeskin-patterned stem and broad, scaly cap. Found in open grassy areas, forest edges, and clearings. Its meaty texture makes it popular for grilling or breading. It’s a reliable find in late summer and autumn.

Raspberry (Rubus idaeus)

Sweet red fruits hanging from thorny canes in summer. Raspberries grow wild along forest margins, hedgerows, and clearings. The berries are nutrient-rich and fragrant, and even the leaves can be used for herbal tea.

Sorrel (Rumex acetosa)

Arrow-shaped leaves with a distinctly sour, lemony taste due to natural oxalates. Found in meadows and pastures, sorrel is excellent in soups and sauces. Its tangy punch brings brightness to wild greens mixes and springtime dishes.

Wild Strawberry (Fragaria vesca)

Small, fragrant berries bursting with flavor, hidden among heart-shaped leaves and white blossoms. Found in forest clearings and sunny slopes. More aromatic and concentrated than cultivated varieties, these berries are a nostalgic favorite among foragers.

Black Truffle (Tuber melanosporum)

A subterranean fungus that grows in symbiosis with oak, hazel, and hornbeam. Found in calcareous soils and collected with trained dogs or pigs. Valued for its deep aroma and culinary luxury, the black truffle is a rare prize of the Mediterranean forest floor.

Walnut (Juglans regia)

Tall tree producing thick-husked nuts that fall in early autumn. Wild walnuts can be found in valleys, old estates, and mixed woodlands. The nuts are rich in oils and protein, stored easily, and used in baking, sauces, and traditional liqueurs.
